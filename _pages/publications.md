## Working Papers

<div class="paper-entry">
<p><strong>Just Phaseout: How International Climate Finance drives Indonesia's Energy Transition</strong></p>
<p class="paper-meta">Solo-authored</p>
<details><summary>Abstract ▸</summary>
<p>Energy transitions are costly, especially in the Global South. Can North-South climate finance spur energy transitions, and under what domestic conditions? I study this in the context of the Just Energy Transition Partnership (JETP) — a landmark deal launched in Indonesia in 2022 that commits $20 billion to accelerate energy transitions. I examine the effects of JETP using a hard-to-abate yet consequential case: coal phaseout in Indonesia. I build a spatially granular panel dataset of every coal plant's operational status and nearby air quality as a proxy for local harms each plant causes. I use a difference-in-differences design to evaluate JETP's early impact on phase-out across plants with varying local pollution levels. I find that after JETP's announcement, coal plants in more polluted areas are significantly more likely to be phased out. Notably, Indonesian policymakers prioritized closing highly polluting coal plants after JETP to signal commitment to international donors rather than merely prioritizing the oldest or cheapest units. Policymakers seem to strategically signal to donor countries by retiring plants that have been more widely searched among donor countries, from Google Trends data. This study challenges skeptical views that climate aid yields little change at the country-level and shows a more complex domestic-international interaction in the political economic drivers of phaseout.</p>
</details>
</div>

<div class="paper-entry">
<p><strong>Paris Agreement Polarizes U.S. Local Policymakers on Climate Action</strong></p>
<p class="paper-meta">with Jonathan Chu</p>
<details><summary>Abstract ▸</summary>
<p>What motivates local policymakers to support climate action? We argue that international treaties like the Paris Agreement can, counterintuitively, undermine domestic climate governance by activating partisan identities. In a survey experiment with 523 local elected officials, we find that invoking the Paris Agreement significantly reduces support for climate action among Republicans, worsening partisan polarization. Democratic policymakers are unaffected. Alternative frames — including the "Climate Mayors" network — had no effect on either group. These results suggest that local political context and partisan identity moderate the relationship between international commitments and domestic policy support, with important implications for climate communication strategies aimed at subnational governments.</p>
</details>
<p class="paper-meta"><em>Presented at: AAPOR 79th Annual Conference, 2024</em> &nbsp;|&nbsp; <em>Student Conference Award, AAPOR 2024</em></p>
</div>

<div class="paper-entry">
<p><strong>When is Climate Change a Security Issue? Experimental Evidence from U.S. Public Opinion</strong></p>
<p class="paper-meta">with Jessica Weeks</p>
<details><summary>Abstract ▸</summary>
<p>Under what conditions does the American public view climate change as a matter of national security, and what does that imply for climate policy attitudes? We treat securitization as a measurable public perception with the potential to affect policy support. We further theorize that securitization is more successful in the aftermath of major climate shocks that make climate impacts feel more urgent and proximal, heighten threat-related emotions, and raise perceived risk — conditions that also increase receptivity to elite security framing. We field two preregistered studies. Study 1, fielded in 2025, develops and validates a securitization index capturing whether respondents see an issue as implicating sovereignty, international power, military responsibility, and direct security threats. Study 2, planned for 2026, combines experimental survey methods with granular geospatial data on wildfire risk and exposure. Using a rolling design during peak wildfire season, we sample adults in high-risk areas and leverage quasi-random variation in wildfire timing to compare respondents who experience an actual wildfire to respondents in the same general area who do not. We test whether wildfire exposure and elite security framing increase securitization, and we explore how securitization relates to pro-climate attitudes and support for climate mitigation and adaptation policies.</p>
</details>
<p class="paper-meta"><em>Presented at: World Congress of Political Science, 2025; Environmental Politics and Governance Annual Conference, 2026</em></p>
</div>

<div class="paper-entry">
<p><strong>How Psychological Distance shapes Americans' Climate Change Preferences: a Conjoint Analysis</strong></p>
<p class="paper-meta">with Wanying Wei and Wei-Ting Tsai</p>
<details><summary>Abstract ▸</summary>
<p>How does the psychological distance of natural disasters influence climate attitudes? Existing work that applies construal level theory (CLT) to study climate perceptions reports mixed effects, precluding clear messaging guidance for climate change communicators. We argue that prior experimental evidence mostly does not test distance dimensions independently, despite CLT being a multidimensional theory comprising social, spatial, temporal, and probabilistic distance. We contribute a systematic test of the effects of psychological distance on climate attitudes by independently manipulating each distance dimension via a pre-registered conjoint experiment about floods. Study 1 recruited 700 Republicans, finding that socially proximate natural disasters that affect one's ingroup consistently increase pro-climate attitudes. However, spatial, temporal, and probabilistic distance generally do not have a significant effect. Specifically, highlighting the impacts of climate on social ingroups can increase Republicans' pro-climate attitudes by up to 5 percentage points. Our proposed Study 2 applies a national sample, validates our manipulations, and tests theoretically relevant mechanisms — including ingroup identification strength, moral foundations, and affective responses — that may account for the outsized effects of social distance.</p>
</details>
<p class="paper-meta"><em>Presented at: MPSA 2025; SPSA 2026</em></p>
</div>
